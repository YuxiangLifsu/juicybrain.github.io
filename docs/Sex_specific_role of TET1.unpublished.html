Epigenetic regulation of drug addiction

Illicit drug abuse poses problems to all societies in multiple facets, including human health and medical expenses, social security and stability, workforce and productivity. Intriguingly, only about 10% to 16% of humans become addicted to drugs. Though we know that environmental, genetic and epigenetic risk factors contribute to the aetiology, the underlying molecular mechanism is still elusive due to the anatomical characterization of the human brain. Utilizing mouse behaviour models, bioinformatic techniques and Low-cell number NGS sequencing tools such as whole-genome bisulfite sequencing, ATAC-seq, Hi-C and RNA-seq, I am investigating drug addiction-related epigenetic alterations in cell type-specific neurons of drug addiction-related brain regions.

Biological sex difference and Epigenetic mechanisms

These discrepancies in drug addiction can be attributed to multiple factors, including social and cultural environments. Historically, illicit drugs have been more accessible to males, contributing to the sex-biased prevalence. Meanwhile, biological factors stem from each individual's genome. In addition to sex chromosomes, which guide sexual development and result in distinct sex hormones, epigenetic mechanisms play a crucial role at the interface between genes, environment, and personal experiences. DNA methylation, an essential epigenetic pathway, involves the addition of a methyl group to the 5-position of cytosine in the genome by DNA methyltransferases. The active turnover of methylated cytosine is facilitated by the DNA dioxygenase enzymes known as Ten-eleven translocation (TET) enzymes. Previous studies have indicated that neuronal activity and cocaine use can lead to a loss of function of TET1 in neurons. In this study, we discovered that deletion of TET1 in the Nucleus accumbens (NAc) dopamine receptor Drd1-expressing medium spiny neurons (D1-MSNs) leads to sex-specific cocaine addiction-like behaviors in both males and females. Notably, DNA methylation and 3D genome organization profiling suggest that TET1-dependent, sex-specific epigenomic features could underlie these sex-specific behavioral phenotypes. Our research highlights TET1's sex-specific roles in cocaine addiction and the associated sex-dimorphic epigenomic modifications in a key neuronal population.
